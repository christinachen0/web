Mediums

Acrylic paint was developed in the 1950's. The pigment is suspended in acrylic polymer emulsion has the characteristic of rubber. It is very fast drying, and will not decolor or crack over time. The essentials for acrylic painting include a palette, a palette knife for blending, brushes that are marked as approved for acrylic paint, a canvas, water, and paper towels.

  • Beginner's set

Some general rules to keep in mind when painting with acrylics:

  • Progress from mid/light tones to darker colors.
  • Begin creating large shapes then move to small details. You want to create a sort of "base" before going into finer details. Try to study the reference picture and see what colors you can pick up.
  • Keep in mind that acrylics dry FAST!! You want to try to work relatively fast and most blending should be done on your palette. You can try to reactivate the paint by using a little water from a spray bottle.

Oil paint is considered the most versatile medium because you can thin it out or built up in more vibrant layers. One advantage of oil paint is that it does not dry as easily, which gives the artist time to blend or fix mistakes.

    Note: The pigment in oil paint often contains hazardous chemicals that can irritate your skin if you touch it. Wear gloves and other protective clothing when using oil paint.

Oil paint dries very very slowly. So, if you think the surface is dry, the paint underneath can still be wet. You must be very careful. Also, a golden rule is to paint thin swatches of paint first and then layer slowly. After you are done, you must add a layer of an oil-based medium to prevent it the paint from cracking in the future!

Styles

One of my favorite styles is abstract art. Abstract artists freely uses color, shapes, forms, and gestural art to represent an idea. It is up to the viewer to interpret what the art conveys. Famous abstract artists include Kandinsky, Pollock, and Rothko.

Another type of style is called realism. Realism emerged in France in the 1850s. Realism artists focsed on painting the average, working class people, contemporary setting, and depicted scenes just as they saw them. Important leading figures include Gustave Courbet.
